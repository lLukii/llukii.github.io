Growing up, I was always praised for having self-awareness. "Wow, you are a great reflector, Lucas. You'll do great things!" my parents told me. Though I might've had good conscience as a kid, I recently realized that I haven't benefited a lot from it when I was younger. Take this anecdote as an example:


Back when I was in 7th grade, I slacked on an English project so much that I didn't even begin working on it until the day of presentations. As you can probably expect, I got a flat zero. Feeling horribly guilty afterwards, I sent my teacher an email saying how sorry I was and that I would lock in afterwards. To my surprise, instead of scolding me, she complimented my apology letter, noting that I was a "very conscientious student". After reading her reply, I essentially abandoned all the guilt I felt previously and continued through the year playing video games in class; instead of changing my work ethic, I remained the class delinquent. So, this got me thinking: is conscience an overrated trait that parents glorify, and what value does it truly bring?


Picture yourself in the shoes of someone who just realized deep down that skipping classes every other day was a *terrible idea*; they'd probably feel like a complete and utter failure. In these moments of despair, it's incredibly easy for people to latch on to anything to make themselves feel better, and praising your conscience seems a perfect way of doing so. But, we all follow a positive feedback loop: once you're praised for your self-awareness, it makes you feel good. In turn, that compliment would encourage you to think that you are fine. This is exactly what happened to me: I quickly associated my English teacher's compliment with the idea that I was fine the way I was, even though I wasn't exactly in the best spot for a seventh grader (as I would find out upon returning to the States in 8th grade).


My point is that praising conscience has a contradictory effect: it makes you feel empowered, but this confidence booster also frames conscience as a destination rather than a preliminary step, essentially defeating its purpose. Now, I'm not trying to say that conscience is inherently bad; you need to have moral awareness to implement the necessary actions for change. But, conscience shouldn't be presented in a way to reward instant gratification, but rather to amplify its judgment. If someone feels like they did something horribly wrong, you should propel them to turn their mere awareness into reality. Of course, this response is harder to grapple with at first. But, in the long run, it's infinitely better to tackle the truth head-on and eventually realize your growth than to remain in stagnancy.


Apparently, a quick Google search reveals that this is a well-documented phenomenon called [Self-Licensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-licensing). I guess I shouldn't be surprised that this term has already been coined. After all, there are over 8 billion people on this planet, and what do I know about philosophy/psychology??
