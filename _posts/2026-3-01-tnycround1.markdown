I was horribly demotivated waking up Sunday morning to do TNYC considering I had FTC Superqualifiers, which knocked me to my knees out of exhaustion, and a sour stomach for nearly two hours right when I went to bed. But of course, I spent all of september through november working on this project, so I wasn't going to drop it *that* easily.

I got to Stuyvesant HS (yes, TNYC was held here) at around 10:45 AM and quickly set up my poster on floor 6 where all the CBIO kids were. Before coming in, I thought I had a decent project—using Machine Learning to diagnose throat cancer from patient voice data (I got this idea after [Lynja](https://www.youtube.com/c/Lynja) passed away in 2024). Immediately, though, my ass got humbled *big time*. I'm not kidding when I say those kids were locked in like crazy: they were giving each other mocks, providing genuine feedback for each other despite having completely different projects, and just doing really impressive work in general. Oh, and it certainly didn't help that the girl facing my table won an ISEF grand award the year before and worked on some elite ball fluid dynamics model with a whole binder of notes and a full-fledged 3D simulation. Yeah... I'm bloody cooked. 

Contrary to my expectations, the students there quite outgoing, and so a few people approached my poster not too long after I got there. I gave a practice presentation to them, and then checked out on the girl's project facing me. I was pretty much lost for the entire time by wtf was happening (maybe just buzzwords, idk), but she was actually really nice and we ended up talking about random things while waiting for judges to come (e.g., music playlists, school, etc.). Going to an all-boys school, this was probably the most I've ever talked to a girl in a long time. 

Judging was a lot harder than I expected. The first judge came to my table at around 1:00 PM, and suggested a casual conversation instead of a formal presentation. He didn't focus that much on the research topic, and jumped straight into my training/validation loss function curves. He complimented me for having them, but noted that I could've applied early stopping, and that my y-axis was mistakenly labeled as "accuracy" instead of "loss" (oops :skull:). He suggested me to check out some gradient boosting model, and asked for a few confusion matrix plots. I didn't put on my poster, but showed them with some of the (questionable) experimental plans I recorded. Anyways, idr much beyond that. 

The second judge asked for a pretty standard presentation + Q&A format. He stopped almost immediatley after I started to ask why I labeled CT Scans as invasive (in retrospect, ig they aren't, but still not really accessible outside of underserved communities) but I got out of that question fairly quickly by pivoting my project's focus to accessibility. Luckily, the rest of the presentation was pretty smooth sailing. Afterwards, he asked me a few questions about my methodology, and suggested to make the model more multimodal. There was one question where he asked about a PCA diagram I had that lowkey threw me off, but I was able to answer all the other ones ok. 

I will say that the questions were much more technical than I expected, as I anticipated most of them to be about the project's impact rather than technical details (in fact, I was preparing to explain what a training batch was in my notes :skull:). Well, it is computational biology, not health sciences or whatever, so I guess that makes sense. Hopefully I can make it to finals, but I'm not sure about its feasibility given that most projects in CBIO were very sweaty. 

Overall, I had a suprisingly good time at TNYC. I talked to a lot of really cool people and made some friends. In some ways, the crazy ass projects I saw today kinda propelled me to start grinding ML again; it's a relevant field with lots of potential/robust applications, and the projects I saw clearly illustrated that. I might do something related to Reinforcement Learning for my senior distinction, since it relates to robotics/video games a lot. (Watch me add Proximal Policy Optimization to Ultro's robot next year and win control award XD)

Ok, I'm tired after the bloodbath that was this past week. Imma go watch the newest episode of Frieren S2 for some eyebleach.
